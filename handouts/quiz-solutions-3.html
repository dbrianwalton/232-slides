    Solution.

    1. Because \(f(x)\) is a power function, we know \(f(x) = A \cdot x^p\) for constants \(A\) and \(p\text{.}\) We use the points to create equations for \(A\) and \(p\text{.}\)
      \begin{align*} (x,y) = (1, 12) \quad \Rightarrow \quad \amp 12 = A \cdot 1^p \\ \amp 12 = A \\ (x,y) = (8, 3) \quad \Rightarrow \quad \amp 3 = A \cdot 8^p \end{align*}
      Since we know \(A=12\text{,}\) we substitute into the second equation
      \begin{equation*} 3 = 12 \cdot 8^p \end{equation*}
      and then isolate \(8^p\) to get
      \begin{equation*} 8^p = \frac{1}{4} \end{equation*}
      We now recognize that 8 and 4 are both powers of 2, \(8=2^3\) and \(4 = 2^2\) so that we can reach an equation where the exponents must match:
      \begin{gather*} (2^3)^p = \frac{1}{2^2} \\ 2^{3p} = 2^{-2} \\ 3p = -2 \\ p = \frac{-2}{3} \end{gather*}
      ðŸ”—
      We have found \(f(x) = 12 x^{-2/3}\text{.}\)

    2. Now that we know \(f(x) = 12 x^{-2/3}\text{,}\) we can find \(f'(x)\text{:}\)
      \begin{equation*} f'(x) = 12 \cdot (\frac{-2}{3}) x^{-5/3} = -8 x^{-5/3} \end{equation*}
      The exponent is negative, so \(f'(x)\) is undefined at \(x=0\text{.}\) The root (cube root) is odd, so the domain includes both negative and positive values. The power is also odd, so the sign changes at \(x=0\text{.}\) We have
      • For \(x > 0\text{,}\) we have \(f'(x) < 0\) (negative)
        ðŸ”—
        ðŸ”—
      • For \(x < 0\text{,}\) we have \(f'(x) > 0\) (positive)
        ðŸ”—
        ðŸ”—
      So \(f(x)\) is increasing on \((-\infty, 0)\) and decreasing on \((0,\infty)\text{.}\)
      ðŸ”—
      ðŸ”—
    3. From \(f'(x) = -8 x^{-5/3}\text{,}\) we can compute \(f''(x)\text{:}\)
      \begin{equation*} f''(x) = -8(\frac{-5}{3}) x^{-8/3} = \frac{40}{3} x^{-8/3} \end{equation*}
      We are still working with odd (cube) roots, and this time there is an even power, so the sign of \(f''(x)\) is the same on both intervals. For \(x \ne 0\) we have \(f''(x) > 0\) (positive). So \(f(x)\) is concave up on \((-\infty, 0)\) and on \((0,\infty)\text{.}\) (It is not valid to combine these intervals because \(f(x)\) is not continuous at \(x=0\text{.}\)
